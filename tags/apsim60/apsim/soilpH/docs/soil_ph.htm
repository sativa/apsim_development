SoilpH Module Scope

The externally visible functionality of the module is to make available information about changes in pH and Al to crops in a simulation.

In order to achieve this functionality the module will require input values from APSIM modules that deal with the N cycle acidification and C cycle acidification.

 

Nitrogen cycle.

In any closed system there will be no acidification, however cropping systems are open systems. An expression of the model includes reference states, whose charges are irrelevant, ie in the context of the model they are neutral.

 

Reactions within the soil will produce either an acid or an alkali product. Removal of nitrates by leaching leaves hydrogen ions in the soil layer - acidification, whereas removal of ammonium ions by leaching removes hydrogen ions in the soil layer - alkalisation. The leaching is calculated for each layer as soil water flows down through the layers.

 

In practical terms, this means that the module needs to keep a running balance of what is added to and taken from the soil. For example, adding a nitrate fertiliser has an alkalisation effect (binds to hydrogen ions, effectively removing them from the soil equation) whereas adding ammonia fertiliser has an acidification effect (frees hydrogen ions into the soil). Nitrate-based fertilisers are uncommon in Australian agriculture.

 

Based on the above, knowledge of the various fertiliser forms must be available to the module; this requirement is met by (fertiliz.ini).

 

Fertilisers are applied to the topsoil (layer 1) and the rate at which the fertiliser is applied (kg/ha) is converted to a “charge” value. Some of the fertiliser is taken by the plant, and this must be tracked for use in the balance equation, while the rest is leached through the layers. Module calculates the hydrogen charge inputs and outputs for each layer.

 

Since nwheat is the only current crop module that accounts for ammonium ions, it will be a good choice for sensitivity analysis on the errors that might be expected from other crop modules in which it is assumed that all nitrogen is taken up as nitrate and for predictive capability.

 

The existing application SPAM takes, as a parameter, an estimate of the annual rate of acidification and distributes it throughout the soil layers. However, the new module will calculate the acidification value and use SPAM formulae for the distribution. Currently, soilwat does the nitrogen distribution but will not distribute pH, ie pH distribution will not be via the transport mechanism modelled in soilwat. Different minerals move at different rates within the soil, some like carbonate move very slowly, thus adding lime may improve the topsoil but not the subsoil in the short term. Models need to account for the differential rates of movement of solutes within the soil layers.

 

Carbon cycle

Models account for organic acids that are removed by harvesting.

 

The grain of a crop is alkaline, and every crop has a reference value, its ash alkalinity (in cmol/kg). Burning off a crop material burns of nitrogen leaving more of the positively charged cations such as calcium, magnesium etc. than negatively charged anions such as P and S. Plants create organic anions ( - particles) to maintain their internal pH. The net effect can be tracked and measured using the ash alkalinity value. For example a crop with an ash alkalinity of 6 cmol/kg, if we remove 3t/ha of grain the total anions removed is (3x1000) x 6 = 18000 cmol/ha removed.

 

One issue that needs to be addressed the availability of the appropriate ash alkalinity values of crop components (grain, straw, leaf). Since these are an attribute of the crop, they should be made available as constant (calibration) values for the crop. The alternative solution is to include an ash alkalinity look-up table for the SoilAcid module to access (possibly within its own .ini file). This solution does not align well with the current APSIM philosophy of module, self-containment.

 

The other aspect of the carbon cycle is the addition of organic matter, for example residue, that is created on the spot. This affects the soil organic matter and thus the cation exchange capacity (that is equivalent to its exchange acidity). A formula is required for the addition of both fresh and humic matter to the soil.

 

Fresh plant material has a plant pH of about 6.5. Its final pH, after degradation, is that of oxalic acid, 1.5. The pH changes along a gradient, the function of that includes a value A plus the pH.

 

In fresh matter, A is variable whereas pH is constant. In humic matter, A is set constant at 32 and the pH is variable (32 x [pH top layer - 1.5]).

 

The increase in the two organic matter fractions that SoilAcid uses are available as deltas from the residue module.

 

Tracking acid rain

In Australia, there is little interest in the topic of acid rain, however this is not the case in the northern hemisphere, and given the increasing global nature of simulation environments it is important that it be considered in this new module.

 

Currently no module tracks the pH of rain, and that information is not available in standard climate files. However, the pH of unpolluted rain is in the range of 5.6 - 5.2 and has the potential to affect soil pH and the rate of transport of solutes though the profile. The current APSIM modules are implicitly calibrated with unpolluted (ie acidic rain) on alkaline soils. SPAM includes functionality to handle acid rain. This will be included in the new module, but the prototype will be parameterised with an average rain pH.

 

Feeback loop for buffering effects on pH

Buffering affects the proportion of H + ions that, when added to a soil, change its acidity. For example in a poorly buffered soil (eg sand that has a low cation exchange capacity) the addition of x H + ions will cause the acidity to move by an equivalent x. However, in a highly buffered soil the acidity will move by x-n. Some attributes of a soil that can affect this:

  1. total cation exchange capacity
  2. organic matter
  3. presence of aluminium - as soil becomes more acid the proportion of Al increases, with a consequent increase in buffering but total cation exchange capacity diminished with a corresponding decrease in buffering. Eventually the level of Al prevents any further increase in soil acidity.

These three factors change over time. The feedback loop accounts for the effects on soil, over time, as conditions change. The currently implemented version of SPAM does not have Al but it has been included in a later model

 

Effects of Manganese

The effects of manganese events are seasonal, short-lived and leave the soil in the same buffered/acidic state as before the event, their effects on the soil are negligible relative to the long-term effects that are to be studied (ie 20 year studies). This module does not include manganese modelling.

 

General

Both pH and Al have the potential to affect a crop, too much Al and the crop is poisoned.

Different ways of measuring pH. Most general method in Australia is in Calcium Chloride (a buffered solution which is useful for diagnostic purposes) however in Qld it is usually measured as pH in water, which is accurate for modelling root growth but less useful for diagnostic purposes. The type if initial pH (water or CalCl) needs to be known to the SoilAcid module and if type = water, a conversion to CalCl done.

SoilpH Module History

SPAM is currently implemented in CSMP. The implementation contains a single macro HORIZN that ‘groups' calculations containing references to vectors of layer parameters. The macro group plus the remaining calculations are invoked once per time step. The SPAM code also includes model constant parameters and parameters for the soil profile vectors. Additional input information for a model run can be extracted from the HORIZN macro instantiations contained in the code.

 

Implementations of the definitive, reference version of SPAM are available in both standard FORTRAN 77 and Borland Delphi languages.

SoilpH Module Dependencies

  • dlt_caco3. The fertiliz module is the one that applies lime to the soil.
  • dlt_OM is obtained from the SoilN2 module.
  • hum_c, fom_c and biom_c are obtained from the soiln module in order to obtain the fraction of organic carbon.
  • crop_type, no3_uptake, nh4_uptake, rlv and root_length. These variables are obtained from each crop in order to know the uptakes of NO 3 - , NH 4 + , and estimate the uptakes of P, S Mg, Cl, K, Na, and Ca. Currently, the only crops that supplies these are Nwheat, Sugar and crops derived from Legume.
  • Infiltration and flow_water. These are obtained from the water movement module soilwat2 or Apswim, in order to know how much water is moving between each layer.
  • dlayer. This is obtained from the water movement module soilwat2.
